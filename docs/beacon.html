Movie Review on Turbo (2013)
By Colin O'Donnell

MOVIE REVIEW

“Turbo”: High-Octane Clash Between an Energized Snail & F1 Cars

David Soren’s epic tale of an ordinary snail defying all odds



Where to Watch: Netflix, Amazon Prime Video ($3.99), VUDU ($7.99)

Watch Trailer

Title: Turbo

Director: David Soren

Genres: Comedy, Action, Animation

MPAA Rating: PG

Duration: 1h 36min

Producer(s): DreamWorks Animation

Ever dreamt of a turbo-charged snail? Gifted the powers of nitrous, Theo the Snail competes against lightning-fast F1 race cars. Nicknamed Turbo, the slimy specimen is about to redefine what it means to go at a ‘snail’s pace.’ “Turbo” boasts constant action and spectacular visuals that make the audience jump out of their shells. Additionally, with “Turbo’s” status as a classic children’s movie, the viewer is absorbed by a sense of nostalgia. This review will dive into what makes the movie “Turbo” so enjoyable.

“Turbo” was released by DreamWorks Animation on July 17, 2013. The film's director, David Soren, helped produce the movie along with many talented actors and animators. David Soren is also known for creating “Merry Madagascar” (2009) and “Captain Underpants: The First Epic Movie” (2017). Additionally, Soren has contributed to works such as “Chicken Run” (2000) and “Shark Tale” (2004) as a story artist.

The film begins by zeroing in on the life of a hopeless snail, Theo. Theo turns on the TV every morning to watch the F1 rallies. He daydreams racing alongside them, but remembers he is only a slow-moving snail. One evening, Theo ventures to a bridge across the highway to witness a drag race between two pimped-out muscle cars. As Theo gazes at the contest, a strong gust of wind blows him off the bridge. Theo plummets into the engine of a muscle car and the nitrous swarms his nervous system.

The nitrous allows Theo to move at tremendous rates. Theo speeds off around town until he meets a man named Tito who races snails. Tito, amazed by Theo’s unbelievable ability, takes Theo to race in the Indy 500. During the most influential race of the year, the astonished fans gave Theo the nickname Turbo. Then, Turbo outspeeds and outmaneuvers the reigning champ, Guy Gagné, to collect his well-deserved trophy.

What makes “Turbo” special is the plot and the wide cast of popular actors. Famous actor Ryan Reynolds appears as Theo (Turbo) in the film. Reynolds has also starred in movies like “Deadpool”, “Deadpool 2” and the “Fast & Furious” series. However, in the production of “Turbo”, blockbuster actors like Ryan Reynolds, Samuel L. Jackson, Bill Hader and Maya Rudolph are not the most fascinating aspects of the film. To the audience’s surprise and mine, personalities like rapper Snoop Dogg and comedian Ken Jeong emerge in the movie. Rather than these popular celebrities playing short-side characters, they play significant roles in the plot. These casting additions are what make ‘Turbo’ so unique. Producers, DreamWorks Animation, not only delivered with recognizable actors but also the soundtrack as well.

While “Turbo” is not remembered for the soundtrack, the film does include some hit, nostalgic tracks. For example, “Turbo” features ‘Drop it Like it’s Hot' by Snoop Dogg and the classic ‘Eye of the Tiger’ written by Survivor. Possibly the most engaging song of the film was a score produced by DreamWorks Animation itself. As Theo enters Tito’s Mexican restaurant, the song ‘Krazy’ resonates throughout the scene. The song’s jumpy vibe creates a fun atmosphere for the viewers. DreamWorks never fails to bring quality animations/visuals to the film industry, and they do not disappoint with “Turbo”. DreamWorks’ attention to vivid imagery and fast-paced scenes stands out. Every frame was meticulously crafted to immerse the viewer in the high-speed journey.

The animation and graphics of “Turbo” are too captivating not to gloss over. The fast-paced animations flawlessly convey the excitement and energy of the racing sphere. For this reason, “Turbo” was nominated for multiple awards.

“Turbo” grossed $282 million at the box office. DreamWorks doubled its original budget. The movie was nominated six times for the Annie Awards, which represent the top animation films of the year. Although “Turbo” didn’t win, the project was elected for ‘Best Animation/Family Trailer’. Finally, “Turbo” won during the Black Reel Awards. Samuel L. Jackson received a prize for ‘Best Voice Performance’. While “Turbo’s” collection of awards is minimal compared to other lucrative films, the masterpiece deserved more. ‘Turbo’ is an underrated film for those who want to experience a movie rich in exceptional animation and sentimental feeling.

Despite “Turbo's” satisfactory first half of the film, the finale seems lackluster. The antagonist, Guy Gagné, is one-dimensional. To start, Guy Gagné has a limited backstory. There is no real introduction to him besides the fact that Turbo looks up to him and that he is the best F1 racer. A second complaint is that Guy Gagné appears on screen for only a few moments in the film. He appears on the scene for at most seven minutes. Even though this lack of an antagonist seems detrimental to the film, it is easy to look past the absence of Guy Gagné and respect the rest of the film.

Overall, “Turbo” contains an involved backstory and a decent plot. The sound, visuals, and distinguishable cast bring the entire movie together. However, the absence of a quality antagonist discourages some viewers. “Turbo” was released in 2013. As a result, the film holds a sentimental value for kids born in the 2000s. The movie might not have the same effect on the older generation. The lack of nostalgia felt for the film could sacrifice “Turbo’s” enjoyability. Being a kid from the 2000s, “Turbo” is one of my all-time favorite movies. So, “Turbo” warrants a rating of 9.8/10.
